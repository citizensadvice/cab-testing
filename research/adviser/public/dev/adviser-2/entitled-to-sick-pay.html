Check if you're entitled to sick pay

This advice is for

With many jobs, if you’re off sick for a few days, you still get paid. This is because you have contractual sick pay. Your contract should tell you what you’re entitled to. If you haven’t been given a contract or it’s not in there, ask your employer or check your staff handbook.

This page mostly covers statutory sick pay. Get more detail on contractual sick pay.

Not all employers offer contractual sick pay, but there’s also statutory sick pay (SSP). All employers have to give you this, by law, as long as you:

  • have done at least one day’s work for your employer
  • are sick for 4 full days or more in a row (including non-working days)
  • earn on average more than £112 a week (before tax)

You still get it if you’re an agency or casual worker, or if you work part-time or on a fixed-term or zero hours contract.

SSP can give you £88.45 a week for up to 28 weeks.

You’ll need to follow some rules to get sick pay you’re entitled to.

Who is entitled to statutory sick pay

Most people who work can get SSP. Entitlement is based on social security law, rather than employment law.

Your client is entitled to SSP if they work and on average earn more than £112 a week - enough for their employer to pay secondary class 1 national insurance contributions. This includes agency workers and office holders, such as police officers, who may not qualify for other employment rights.

When you can get statutory sick pay

Your client has to be in a ‘period of incapacity for work’ to get SSP. This means being sick for at least 4 days in a row - including non-working days such as weekends and public holidays.

In a period of incapacity for work, your client will only get SSP for days when they'd be working if they weren't sick. These are called 'qualifying days'.

There's no SSP for the first 3 qualifying days of a period of incapacity for work. These are called 'waiting days'.

Your client must have the whole day off sick for it to count. If they go off sick partway through the day it only counts if they did no work before they left.

How much statutory sick pay is paid

For any full week your client has off work they will get the full weekly rate of £88.45, even if they work part-time.

They will get a daily rate of SSP for the first and last weeks of their sickness if they’re not full weeks.

You can use the statutory sick pay calculator on GOV.UK to work out exactly what they’ll get, or work it out manually using guidance on GOV.UK.

How long statutory sick pay is paid for

SSP is paid for up to 28 weeks. This can be either:

  • continuous
  • several periods of sickness added together to make up 28 weeks - across a maximum of 3 years

Once the client has claimed for 28 weeks or reached the 3-year limit they can apply for Employment and Support Allowance (ESA).

If a client has been off sick continuously or a number of times over a long period they may have a disability and you should check for any disability discrimination issues.

If your client is off sick more than once

If your client is off sick more than once, it will count as a single period of incapacity for work if the absences:

  • are each 4 or more days in a row
  • take place within 8 weeks

In this situation the client doesn’t have to wait 3 days to get paid SSP again.

If the absences are more than 8 weeks apart the client will start a new period of incapacity, and have to wait the 3 days before getting SSP.

Who isn’t entitled to statutory sick pay

You won’t get SSP if you:

  • are self-employed
  • have already had SSP for 28 weeks
  • are getting statutory maternity pay or Maternity Allowance
  • are pregnant and your baby is due in 4 weeks or less
  • had a baby in the last 14 weeks (or the last 18 weeks if your baby was born over 4 weeks early)
  • are an agricultural worker (read about agricultural sick pay on GOV.UK)
  • are in the armed forces

Even if your employer says you're 'self-employed', you might in fact be a 'worker' and entitled to sick pay. It’s always best to check if this applies to you - contact your nearest Citizens Advice if you’re not sure.

When you have more than one employerAdd reference

If you have more than one employer you could be entitled to sick pay from each one. For each employer, the rules are the same as if they were your only employer.

If your illness means you can do one of your jobs, but not the other, you could get sick pay from one while getting your normal wages from another.

If your employer refuses to pay your sick pay

If you’re not getting the pay your contract says you’re entitled to, you can take steps to get the money you’re owed.

If you’re entitled to statutory pay but your employer refuses to pay it, you should contact HM Revenue and Customs (HMRC).

HMRC employees’ enquiry line
Telephone: 0300 200 3500
Textphone: 0300 200 3212
Open Monday to Friday, 8am to 5pm.

Calls can cost up to 12p a minute from landlines, and between 3p and 45p a minute from mobiles.

If you need more help at any stage, contact your nearest Citizens Advice

What to do if you’re not entitled to sick payAdd reference

If your employer says you’re not entitled to sick pay, ask them to give you a written explanation of their reasons. They should give you this on a form called Statutory sick pay and an employee’s claim for benefit (SSP1). They should give you this within 7 days of you going off sick.

You’ll need the SSP1 form to claim benefits. If you can’t get sick pay, you might be able to claim Employment and Support Allowance and other benefits. Use our benefits checker to see what you might be entitled to.

If your employer doesn’t give you form SSP1 there are 2 steps to take:

Step 1: request a written statementAdd reference

If your employer hasn’t given you form SSP1, ask them for a written statement explaining why you can’t get Statutory Sick Pay. You could also give them a copy of the form to fill in.

Step 2: contact HMRCAdd reference

If you can’t get form SSP1 or a written statement from your employer, contact HMRC’s employees' enquiry line on 0300 200 3500. They’ll ask your employer why they think you’re not entitled to SSP.

If your client works part-time

Every day your client works is a qualifying day, even if they only work for a couple of hours.

If your client works a few days each week, or different days each week, they will have to agree with their employer which days count as qualifying days.

This will affect how long they have to be off before they start getting sick pay, as well as how much sick pay they get for each day they miss.

It can be difficult to work out the best conditions, so you might need to get specialist help.

Example

The employer agrees that only normal working days are qualifying days

Frida works Mondays and Tuesdays. She agrees with her employer that Mondays and Tuesdays will be her qualifying days.

She falls ill on Tuesday 1 November. Her sick pay won't start till Monday 14 November.

This is because her waiting days will be the Tuesday she falls ill and the next Monday and Tuesday.

She'll get the full rate of SSP - half the weekly rate for each Monday or Tuesday she's sick, as her working week is 2 days.

The employer agrees that all weekdays are qualifying days

Jan works Mondays and Tuesdays. He agrees with his employer that all weekdays are qualifying days.

He falls ill on Tuesday 1 November. His sick pay will start on Friday 5 November - his waiting days will be Tuesday to Thursday of that week.

He'll get the full rate of SSP. He'll get one-fifth of the weekly rate for each weekday he's sick - not just the days he'd normally work.

If your client is pregnant or has recently given birth

Their entitlement to sick pay will vary depending if:

  • the illness is related to their pregnancy or not
  • they’re claiming statutory maternity pay or maternity allowance

Read more on the page ‘Getting sick pay when you’re pregnant’.

If your client is also getting their pension

If your client works and claims their state pension, they're still entitled to get SSP as long as they qualify.

If they don’t qualify for SSP, they might be able to get pension credit and other benefits. You can use the benefits checker to see what else they might be entitled to.

If your client is in their notice period

If your client is sick they should get their full normal pay, rather than their statutory or contractual amount, if:

  • they're in their statutory notice period because they’re leaving their job
  • their notice period is the statutory minimum (or less)
  • they haven't resigned

This applies no matter what sick pay terms are specified in your client's contract.

If your client is involved in a trade dispute

If your client is off work because of a trade dispute when their sickness starts, they won’t be entitled to SSP. They might be able to claim ESA instead.

Your client is entitled to SSP if:

  • the sickness started before the trade dispute began
  • they’re laid off because of a trade dispute elsewhere (that they are not directly involved in)

If your client has 2 contracts with the same employer

Depending on how their employer pays their national insurance (NI) contributions, if your client has 2 separate contracts with the same employer they will get:

  • SSP from each job, if the employer’s NI contributions are paid separately for each contract
  • SSP once, if the earnings from each contract are added together to calculate the employer’s NI contributions

If their earnings are added together for NI contributions they have to be unable to work under all contracts to get SSP.

If your client’s employer is insolvent

If the employer is insolvent and can’t pay your client SSP they’re owed then:

  • the employer is liable to pay any SSP due for the period before the insolvency date
  • if the contract ends with insolvency, entitlement to SSP ends at the same time - the employer should give the client form SSP1 so they can claim ESA from DWP
  • if the contract doesn’t end, any SSP due from the insolvency date is HMRC’s responsibility
