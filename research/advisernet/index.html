    What this document is aboutAdd reference

    1This document is about:-

    • how to check whether a client is liable for a debt
    • what to do if s/he is not liable
    • what to do if s/he is in dispute or has a counterclaim
    • what to do if the debt is unenforceable.

    On what grounds can debts be challengedAdd reference

    2Debts can be challenged on the grounds that:-

    • the client is not liable and therefore does not owe the money
    • the amount is wrong
    • the client has a dispute
    • the creditor cannot enforce the debt.

    More about challenging debts in England and Wales - CPAG Debt Advice Handbook

    What is liability for a debtAdd reference

    3A person liable for a debt has a legal duty to pay the amount owed. A person can be liable for the debt on her/his own or with others. Being liable with others is called joint and several liability.

    4What makes a person liable may be different for each debt. A person can be liable:-

    • because of a statutory duty, for example, for council tax; or
    • because s/he has signed a contract, for example, a credit card agreement. This includes signing as a guarantor for someone else; or

      More about consumer contracts
      More about consumer contracts Scotland
    • by her/his conduct even without signing a contract, for example, by using a service such as ordering from a catalogue, or fuel supply; or
    • following a court order, for example, to pay a fine or compensation or damages.

    Joint and several liabilityAdd reference

    5Joint and several liability arises when two or more people are liable for the same debt. Each person is liable for the entire amount of the debt, not just a portion, regardless of what they may have agreed with each other. This means that the creditor may demand repayment of the total amount from any of the liable people.

    6A person can be jointly and severally liable with someone else if:-

    • both or all have signed the agreement; or
    • for some debts, the parties are a couple who are married, civil partners or living together as husband and wife or as civil partners, for example, council tax and water charges.

    GuarantorsAdd reference

    7A guarantor is someone who signs an agreement to say s/he will pay the amount owed if the person who originally signed fails to pay. The guarantor is bound by the terms of the guarantee s/he has given, which usually means s/he will be liable if the borrower defaults.

    More about guarantors and credit agreements

    People who may not be liable for a debtAdd reference

    MinorsAdd reference

    8A minor is someone aged under 18 in England, Wales and N. Ireland and under 16 in Scotland. A creditor cannot usually take court action to recover a debt owed by a minor. This is because a minor cannot make a legally enforceable contract.

    9In England, Wales and N. Ireland, the only exception to this is if the contract is for ‘necessaries’. Although there is no standard definition of what a necessary is, examples include food and clothes.

    10In Scotland, minors aged 16 or over have slightly more ability to make enforceable contracts, but some contracts with minors may be set aside by the courts.

    More about minors and contracts in England and Wales
    More about minors and contracts in Scotland
    More about minors and contracts in N. Ireland

    AgentsAdd reference

    11An agent collecting money on behalf of someone else, for example, for a catalogue company, is not responsible for a customer's unpaid debts provided the agent has opened a separate account for the customer. Most agents’ agreements specify that this should be done. If the agent has not opened a separate account, s/he may be liable for the debt.

    Spouses and partners of a debtor who has diedAdd reference

    12Spouses and partners are not automatically liable for a deceased person’s debts although they may have a joint and several liability. Creditors can attempt to recover any monies outstanding from the deceased person’s estate, including a share in any property.

    More about what happens to debts after death

    What a client can do if s/he is not liableAdd reference

    14If a client thinks s/he is not liable for a debt, s/he needs to:-

    • contact the creditor and tell them why s/he is not liable, for example, because s/he did not sign the contract. The client should ask the creditor to provide documentary proof of liability
    • provide any evidence or further details. For example, if the client is not liable for council tax because s/he didn’t live at the address, s/he should provide evidence to show s/he lived somewhere else at the time
    • if a claim has been issued in court, the client will need to defend the claim

      More about responding to a court claim in England and Wales
      More about responding to a court claim in Scotland
    • in England, Wales and Scotland, if there is already a court order, the client may be able to apply for the order to be set aside.

      More about setting aside a court order in England and Wales
      More about setting aside a court order in Scotland

    The debt is covered by payment protection insurance (PPI)Add reference

    Mortgage and credit payments are often covered by payment protection insurance (PPI). The terms of the insurance cover contained in the policy document should be checked to see what cover is provided and how to claim. Usually there is a time scale within which a policy holder must make a claim.

    16Payment protection insurance (PPI) is commonly taken out with mortgages, loans, credit/store cards or catalogue payments. It is sometimes known as accident, sickness and unemployment (ASU) insurance, ‘Account Cover’ or ‘Payment Cover’.

    17Depending on the individual insurance contract, cover may apply where the policy holder has an accident or sickness, or becomes unemployed through no fault of her/his own. If the policy holder is covered, the insurance company will pay the monthly repayments (or a percentage of them) on behalf of the policy holder for a fixed period of time.

    18Many clients have taken out PPI but find they are not covered because the insurance was mis-sold, for example, because the client was already unemployed or sick. The client may need to cancel the policy and may wish to claim a refund of premiums in the event of mis-selling.

    More about payment protection insurance

    Reasons why a debt may be unenforceableAdd reference

    22If a debt is unenforceable, it means that the creditor cannot take action against the client to make her/him pay. If the creditor issues court proceedings for the debt, the client may have a defence on the grounds that the debt is unenforceable.

    23A debt may be unenforceable where:-

    • the creditor has not met the requirements of the Consumer Credit Act 1974
    • the creditor has not taken court action within the time limit
    • it can be shown that the client did not have the capacity to enter into an agreement
    • it can be shown that the client entered into the agreement under undue influence

    24The following table shows for each type of debt why it might be unenforceable and what to check:-

    Type of debtReason why it is unenforceableWhat to check
    Any creditor has not taken court action within the time limit set down in law
    • when did the debt arise? Was it about or more than six years ago (five years in Scotland)
    • for mortgage debts, if the debt is about or more than 12 years old (20 years in Scotland) is the creditor a member of the council of mortgage lenders
    • when did the client last make a payment or contact the creditor
    • does the creditor have any property belonging to the client or owe the client money
    Consumer credit debts creditor has not met the requirements of the Consumer Credit Act 1974
    • was the agreement signed by both parties
    • were the correct number of copies issued
    • does the unfair relationship test apply
    • was payment protection insurance included when it should not have been
    • was the prescribed information included
    • was the APR included
    Contracts including mortgages and credit agreements the client lacked capacity to enter an agreement
    • did the client understand what she was doing when the contract was made
    Mortgages and contracts generally the client entered the agreement under undue influence
    • did someone else pressure the client to sign the contract

    The creditor has not met the requirements of the Consumer Credit Act 1974Add reference

    25If the creditor has not met the requirements of the Consumer Credit Act 1974, the client may be able to defend a claim against her/him on the grounds that the agreement is unenforceable.

    26Creditors' duties under the Act include rules regarding:-

    • the content of an agreement
    • the supply of copies of an agreement
    • the provision of information regarding cancellation rights.

    26aUntil 1 April 2014, the Consumer Credit Act 1974 also covered credit licensing. From 1 April 2014, the Financial Conduct Authority (FCA) takes over the regulation of consumer credit under the Financial Services and Markets Act 2000, and all firms offering credit or otherwise involved in other regulated activities, need to be FCA authorised.

    More about the FCA authorisation

    26bThe client may be tempted to use a claims management company to help her/him challenge the enforceability of a credit agreement. The Ministry of Justice and what was then Office of Fair Trading warned consumers to seek independent advice before accepting the services of such a company.

    More about whether a credit agreement is enforceable

    27The client may also have a defence under the Unfair Relationship Test.

    More about the Unfair Relationship Test
    More about how to defend a court claim in England and Wales
    More about how to defend a court claim in Scotland

    The creditor has not taken court action within the time limitAdd reference

    If the creditor has not taken court action within the time limit set down in law, the debt is unenforceable. This is known as ‘statute barred’. If the adviser thinks a debt is statute barred, s/he must take advice from a specialist straight away and before contacting the creditor or taking any other action on behalf of the client.

    Time limits in England, Wales and N. IrelandAdd reference

    28In England, Wales and N. Ireland, in general, creditors have six years in which to take a client to court for the debt. There are certain rules about when a time limit starts to run for the purposes of court action for debt.

    More about time limits

    29For the capital sum of a mortgage (not the interest) the time limit is 12 years. However, lenders who are members of the Council of Mortgage Lenders (CML) have agreed voluntarily that they will begin all recovery actions for shortfalls within the first six years following the sale of a property which has been repossessed.

    More about recovery of mortgage arrears in England and Wales
    More about recovery of mortgage arrears in N. Ireland

    30There are no time limits which stop HM Revenue and Customs from collecting tax once the initial assessment has been made.

    More about challenging and negotiating with HM Revenue and Customs

    Enforcement of a judgment in England, Wales and N. IrelandAdd reference

    31In England and Wales, the general rule is that once a judgment has been entered there is no time limit within which to start enforcement proceedings. The Court of Appeal has held that this also applies to charging orders (endnote 1). Therefore, a charging order remains valid even though no steps are taken to enforce the judgment by order for sale.

    32The exception to this rule is that a warrant of control against goods cannot be issued more than six years after a judgment, without leave of the court.

    More about enforcement of money judgments in England and Wales

    33In N. Ireland, the procedure is governed by the Judgments Enforcement (Northern Ireland) Order 1981.

    More about enforcement of money judgments in N. Ireland

    Time limits in ScotlandAdd reference

    34In Scotland, there is a time limit of five years or 20 years for taking legal action to claim money owed. The time limit depends on the type of debt owed. Some of the rules are complicated, for example, if there are arrears of payments of mortgage interest the lender has five years to take action, but where there are arrears of payments on the principal sum (capital) owed under a mortgage, the time limit is 20 years. The rules for time limits for pursuing some debts relating to payments administered by a UK government agency cut across the five or 20 year rules in Scotland. The main examples are:-

    • recovery of overpayments of social security benefits by the DWP through the courts

      More about recovery of overpayment of benefits by the DWP through the courts in Scotland
    • overpayment of housing benefit and council tax benefit, administered by a Scottish local authority

      More about recovery of overpayment of benefits administered by a local authority in Scotland– CPAG Welfare Benefits and Tax Credits Handbook
    • arrears of council tax are recoverable under the 20 year time limit
    • there are no time limits on HM Revenue and Customs collecting unpaid tax, once the initial assessment has been made.

    More about challenging and negotiating with HM Revenue and Customs
    More about recovering mortgage arrears in Scotland
    More about overpayment of benefits
    More about time limits

    Enforcement of a decree in ScotlandAdd reference

    35In Scotland, once a decree has been granted by a court the time limit for it being enforced is 20 years. This includes decisions that have the force of a court decree, for example, an extract registered decree arbitral, a summary warrant for council tax or an extract for enforcement.

    More about enforcing payment of debt in Scotland
    More about recovery of mortgage shortfall debts in Scotland

    When does the time limit beginAdd reference

    36The time limit for taking court action for debt begins from the latest of the following:-

    • the date on which the creditor could have begun action to recover the debt; or
    • the last date on which the debtor made a payment or acknowledged the debt

    37If a client or her/his agent, which could include her/his adviser, makes a payment or acknowledges the debt (admits the debt in writing) within the time limit, time starts running again from the date of the payment or acknowledgement.

    38If a debtor who is jointly and severally liable for the debt makes a payment, the time limit starts running again for both or all the parties. If one of the parties acknowledges the debt in any way other than by a payment, for example in a letter, time only starts running for that person and not for the other jointly and severally liable people.

    39Once the time limit has expired it cannot start up again even if there is a payment or acknowledgement.

    What counts as an acknowledgment of the debtAdd reference

    40A payment always counts as an acknowledgement. The following could also amount to an acknowledgement:-

    • a written request for details of the debt
    • providing details of income and expenditure and an offer of payment, or a letter that states that the debtor cannot afford to make any payment
    • a letter from an adviser with an authority signed by the debtor and referring to 'debt(s)'
    • an admission made in earlier court proceedings, for example, a reply to a summons or claim form.

    41An oral admission of liability, including one made in a telephone call, is not sufficient to be an acknowledgement. A transcript of a telephone call is not evidence of acknowledgment of a debt. If the debt is likely to be statute barred, it is safer to make enquiries by telephone rather than in writing.

    42Making reference to a telephone conversation in a subsequent letter signed by or on behalf of the debtor could allow evidence of the conversation to be given in court, so that the letter and the conversation together could amount to an acknowledgement.

    43A straightforward denial of a debt does not count as an acknowledgment. However, a denial of the amount of a debt, or of part of it, or, of a joint debt, stating that although the debt exists it is a partner or former partner's responsibility, could amount to an acknowledgement.

    Where a debt may be near its time limit, an adviser who contacts a creditor on behalf of a client must be careful not to acknowledge the debt. Marking a letter 'Without Prejudice' will not necessarily protect the client's position.

    If a client is being chased for statute barred debtAdd reference

    43aThe Financial Conduct Authority has published rules and guidance that say:

    • a firm mustn’t demand payment for statute barred debt if the customer has stated they won’t be paying the debt because it’s statute barred
    • a firm shouldn’t threaten a client with court action if the debt is statute barred, because court action is not actually possible
    • a firm mustn’t try to recover a statute barred debt if they haven’t been in contact with the client during the time limit

    43bIt’s important that the client doesn’t acknowledge the debt in writing if the debt isn’t yet statute barred. If they do, the time limit will reset and it will be another 6 years (5 in Scotland) before the debt will be statute barred.

    43cUse the National Debtline’s ’statute barred debt template letter’ to write to the creditor asking to stop contacting the client. The template letter is written in a way that the client will tell the creditor to stop contacting them without acknowledging that they owe them money. It’s best to send the letter by recorded delivery - that way the client can prove the creditor has received it.

    43c

    43dThe client can complain to the Financial Ombudsman if the creditor keeps contacting the client after they’ve sent the template letter, or if the creditor threatens court action for statute barred debt.

    The client did not have the capacity to enter into an agreementAdd reference

    44If the client was not able to understand what s/he was doing when s/he made the agreement, the agreement may not be enforceable on grounds of incapacity. Incapacity could be caused by drink, drugs, mental illness or a mental disability. An agreement can be later ratified when a client has the capacity.

     More about capacity to enter into a contract
     More about capacity to enter into a contract Scotland

    The client entered into the agreement under undue influenceAdd reference

    45If a person has been pressurised, bullied or persuaded by another person with influence over her/him, for example, a member of her/his family, to enter into an agreement which is to her/his detriment, the debt may be unenforceable. If a person is harassed or coerced into entering a credit agreement by a trader, for example, a lender, this is an example of an aggressive commercial practice which is likely to be unfair. The trader could be guilty of a criminal offence.

    More about unfair commercial practices

    Undue influence is a complex area of law and legal advice must be sought in all cases where undue influence may be a factor.

    Fraud and theftAdd reference

    Bank accounts and credit cardsAdd reference

    46If a debt is due to someone using the client’s bank account or credit card details without permission or forging her/his signature, the client should not be held liable for the debt, provided s/he notifies the lender as soon as s/he discover the loss.

    More about theft from a bank account

    47If the client knows who has stolen or used the cheque book or credit card, s/he should report the theft to the police and notify the lender straight away.

    48However, sometimes a fraud is committed by someone known to the client, for example, a family member. In this case, if the client disputes liability for the debt there may be consequences for the third party which the client may need to be aware of.

    Tampering and theft from gas and electricity metersAdd reference

    49A client who has stolen from or tampered with a meter may be prosecuted for theft and will need legal advice. If a client has a debt due to someone else's theft, s/he will not be liable to pay it if s/he can show that s/he took reasonable care and informed the police and the owner of the meter about the theft.

    50If a supplier detects signs of meter tampering, it may remove the meter and disconnect the supply.

    More about theft from and tampering with gas and electricity meters - CPAG Fuel Rights Handbook

    What to do if the client has a defence to a claimAdd reference

    51If a client thinks s/he has grounds to defend a court claim for any reason, s/he should get advice from a specialist adviser.
